 4  The ``zen.algorithms.community`` package provides functions for detecting  
 5  communities of nodes present within a network. Intuitively, a community is  
 6  defined as a group of nodes which are more connected to each other than to the  
 7  rest of the network. The following algorithms are implemented: 
 8   
 9          - **LPA** (Label-Propagation Algorithm) detects communities by assigning  
10          each node a label that the majority of its neighbors agree upon, breaking  
11          ties at random. Its runtime is linear in terms of the number of edges in the 
12          network. However, its use of randomness means that running the algorithm on  
13          the same graph several times can yield different results. It also means that 
14          the algorithm may sometimes fail to return a good solution (i.e. it returns  
15          a single community for the entire network). 
16   
17          - **LabelRank** is a stabilized (deterministic) version of LPA, which also  
18          has parameters to control the propagation of the labels. Like LPA, it has a 
19          linear runtime in terms of edges. Depending on the given parameters, its  
20          memory requirements may be higher than LPA (since it needs to maintain a  
21          table of labels for each node). 
22   
23          - The **spectral modularity** approach maximizes the modularity of the  
24          network using properties of its adjacency matrix. It has a O(n^2 log n)  
25          complexity, where n is the number of nodes in the network. This is worse  
26          than linear complexity in terms of edges. The algorithm only supports  
27          undirected, unweighted networks. 
28   
29          - The **Louvain** algorithm is also a modularity-maximizing algorithm. It 
30          assigns each node to its own community then repeatedly moves nodes to their 
31          neighbors' communities in order to increase the modularity. Its complexity 
32          is linear in terms of nodes.
